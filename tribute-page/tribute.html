Ada Lovelace

The Enchantress of Number, Countess of Lovelace, and......first female computer programmer?

Here’s a brief timeline of Ada Lovelace’s life and legacy:

  • 1815: Born Augusta Ada Byron on December 10, in London, England. She is the daughter of the poet Lord Byron and his wife, Annabella Milbanke.
  • 1816: Her parents separate, and Ada is raised by her mother, Annabella, who encourages her education in mathematics and science to quell any fears about inheriting her father's insanity.
  • 1833: She meets Charles Babbage, a mathematician and inventor, at a party, and becomes fascinated with his work on the Analytical Engine, an early mechanical computer.
  • 1835: Ada marries William King. As he becomes Earl of Lovelace, takes on the title of Countess.
  • 1838: She publishes her first major work, a translation of an article by Italian mathematician Luigi Federico Menabrea on Babbage’s Analytical Engine. Her notes include what is now considered the first algorithm intended to be processed by a machine, earning her the title of the first computer programmer.
  • 1843: Her translation and notes on the Analytical Engine are published, significantly advancing the understanding of computing principles.
  • 1844: Ada’s health begins to decline. She suffers from a series of illnesses, including a suspected case of cancer.
  • 1852: Ada Lovelace dies on November 27, at the age of 36.
  • 1855: Posthumously, her contributions to computing are recognized, but it takes many years for her work to gain wider acknowledgment.
  • 1953: Ada Lovelace is officially recognized for her pioneering work in computer science when her contributions are celebrated in various retrospectives and academic studies.

"Forget this world and all its troubles and if possible its multitudinous Charlatans—every thing in short but the Enchantress of Number." — Charles Babbage

Ada Lovelace valued intuition and imagination. To read more about her story, please visit her Wikipedia entry.
